Inshore vs. Offshore sailing

Same sport, different game. Once you leave the harbour, the rules, the risks, and the mindset all shift. Here's what actually changes.

📅 Published May 2026 ⏱ 8 min read 🏷️ Seamanship
Tap to watch on @_annie_sails →

People often ask me what the difference is between inshore and offshore sailing. On the surface it sounds obvious: one is close to land, the other isn't. But the reality is much more layered than that. The distance from shore changes almost everything about how you prepare, how you sail, and how you think.

I've done both. I started inshore, racing harbour regattas and coastal events. Then I moved offshore, racing up the east coast of Australia. They're both brilliant in their own way, but they ask very different things of you. Let me explain what actually changes.

1. What counts as "inshore" and "offshore"?

There's no universal line on a chart where inshore ends and offshore begins, but the general idea is straightforward.

Inshore racing happens in protected or semi-protected waters. Harbour races, bay races, short coastal sprints. You're usually within sight of land, often within a few nautical miles. Races might last anywhere from 45 minutes to a few hours. You can see the start line, the marks, and usually the finish line from the same spot.

Offshore racing takes you out of sight of land. You're heading from one port to another, often overnight or over multiple days. The Sydney to Gold Coast, for example, is 384 nautical miles. You're out on open ocean, sometimes 30 or 40 miles from the nearest coast, and the race takes two to three days.

There are also coastal races that sit somewhere in between, staying within a few miles of the coastline but covering longer distances. These are sometimes called "passage races" and they share characteristics of both.

2. Preparation

This is where the two start to feel like different sports entirely.

For an inshore race, preparation is about performance. You check the weather forecast, plan your sail selection, maybe tune your rig. You pack a snack and some water. You can forget something and it's annoying, not dangerous.

For an offshore race, preparation is about survival as much as speed. The safety gear list alone runs to several pages. You need life rafts, EPIRBs (emergency position-indicating radio beacons), flares, grab bags, jacklines, tethers, storm sails, and redundant communications. Every boat undergoes a formal safety inspection before the race. Everything gets checked: the structural integrity of the hull, the engine, the electrics, the medical kit.

What's in an offshore safety kit?
Life raft, EPIRB, personal locator beacons, flares (handheld and parachute), fire extinguishers, first aid kit, storm sails, jacklines and tethers, satellite phone or HF radio, AIS transponder, MOB (man overboard) recovery equipment, and a grab bag with emergency water and rations. The full list depends on the race category.

Food planning changes too. Inshore, you eat before or after the race. Offshore, you need to sustain two people for potentially three or four days. That means pre-cooked meals, snacks, hydration planning, and a system for preparing food in a moving galley while the other person drives.

3. Crew and watch systems

Inshore races typically have full crews. On a boat like ours, a Dehler 30 OD, an inshore crew might be four or five people. Everyone has a specific role: helmsman, trimmer, bowperson, tactician. You're all awake, all working, all the time.

Offshore is different. Peter and I sail double-handed, which means just two people for the entire race. You can't both be awake for three days straight, so you rotate in a watch system. One person sleeps while the other sails.

Our typical watch rotation is three hours on, three hours off. The person on watch drives the boat, manages the sails, watches the instruments, keeps a lookout for other vessels, and monitors the weather. The person off watch sleeps, eats, and recovers. In practice, "three hours off" means about two hours of actual sleep once you've eaten, changed layers, and settled in.

This is one of the biggest mental shifts in offshore sailing. You're making decisions alone, at night, when you're tired. There's nobody to confirm your read on the weather or validate a sail change. You have to trust your own judgement.

4. Navigation and strategy

Inshore navigation is largely visual. You can see the marks, you can see other boats, and you can see the wind on the water. Strategy is about reading shifts, positioning against other boats, and executing manoeuvres cleanly. Races are short enough that conditions rarely change dramatically.

Offshore, the strategic picture is far bigger. You're dealing with weather systems that evolve over hours and days. The East Australian Current runs down the coast and can add or subtract knots of boat speed depending on where you position. You're making routing decisions with incomplete information, weighing up whether to stay inshore and risk lighter winds or go wide and pick up current.

Navigation goes from eyeball-and-compass to a full electronic suite: chartplotter, AIS (automatic identification system), radar if you have it, weather GRIB files, and satellite communications. On Alliance, we run a tablet with navigation software, a separate GPS, and backup paper charts.

What changes at night?
Everything feels different in the dark. You navigate by instruments, not by eye. Other vessels become a pattern of navigation lights you need to interpret. Wind shifts are harder to spot because you can't see the water surface. Your depth perception disappears. It takes experience to stay confident and make good calls when you can't see what's happening around you.

5. The sea state

Inshore waters are generally protected. Harbours and bays might have chop, the occasional uncomfortable wave pattern near headlands, but you're rarely dealing with genuine ocean swell.

Offshore, the sea state becomes a major factor. Open ocean swells can run two to four metres on a typical east coast race, and significantly more if a weather system moves through. The boat motion changes completely. You're heeling, pitching, and occasionally getting knocked around by breaking waves. Everything below deck needs to be secured. Moving around the boat requires handholds and conscious foot placement.

This affects everything: how you change sails, how you cook, how well you sleep, how quickly you fatigue. A rough night offshore in a 30-foot boat is physically demanding in a way that inshore racing never is. After a multi-day race, you're properly exhausted.

6. Safety and self-reliance

Inshore, if something goes wrong, help is close. You can retire from a race and motor home. A rescue boat is usually nearby. The worst-case scenario is generally a broken piece of gear or a retirement, not a life-threatening situation.

Offshore, you are your own rescue. If a halyard breaks at 2am, 40 miles from the coast, you fix it. If someone gets injured, you manage it with a first aid kit and a satellite phone call to shore-based medical advice. The nearest help might be hours away by helicopter or half a day by another vessel.

This changes the way you think about every decision. You reef earlier because a blown-out sail offshore is a much bigger problem than it is in the harbour. You double-check every shackle because a failure offshore has consequences. You clip on with a tether every time you leave the cockpit, because falling overboard in the dark is the scenario nobody wants to consider.

It also changes the way you maintain the boat. Before an offshore race, we go through Alliance stem to stern. Every fitting, every line, every piece of electronics. Inshore, a fraying sheet is something you note for next week. Offshore, you replace it before you leave.

7. The mental game

Inshore racing is intense in short bursts. High concentration, fast decisions, constant action. The adrenaline carries you through. It's a sprint.

Offshore racing is a marathon. The intensity comes and goes. There are moments of pure exhilaration, surfing down waves under spinnaker at 15 knots. And there are hours of patience, grinding upwind in light airs at 4 knots, watching the GPS tick over painfully slowly.

The hardest part mentally is the fatigue combined with responsibility. At 3am on night two, when you've averaged four hours of broken sleep a day and the wind has shifted for the fourth time, you still have to make the right call. There's no one to hand over to.

But that's also what makes it deeply rewarding. When you get it right, when you and your co-skipper make smart decisions over three days and cross the finish line knowing you sailed well, it's a feeling that's hard to replicate anywhere else.

So which is better?

Neither. They're complementary.

Inshore racing teaches you boat speed, boat handling, tactical awareness, and how to sail in close quarters. These are foundational skills. Every offshore sailor I respect has an inshore background, because that's where you learn to sail fast and sail well.

Offshore racing teaches you seamanship, self-reliance, weather interpretation, and endurance. It forces you to plan properly, think ahead, and manage yourself as much as you manage the boat.

If you're starting out, start inshore. Learn to sail well in a controlled environment. Build your skills, build your confidence, and build your experience. When the time feels right, start crewing on offshore races. You'll know when you're ready because the idea of being out there won't feel daunting, it'll feel like the next logical step.

For Peter and me, this season is about bringing both together: the boat speed from inshore campaigns and the seamanship from offshore miles. That's the combination that makes a good double-handed team.
