Meet the boat

An introduction to Employment Hero Alliance - our Dehler 30 OD, and the double-handed campaign we're building around her.

📅 Published May 2026 ⏱ 6 min read 🏷️ Campaign
Tap to watch on @_annie_sails →

This is Employment Hero Alliance. She's a Dehler 30 OD - a one-design sportboat built from the ground up for short-handed offshore racing. Thirty feet of carbon rig, deep keel, and twin rudders. She's not a cruiser someone bolted a racing sail onto. She was designed to do exactly what we're doing with her: go offshore, go fast, with two people.

I want to explain why this boat, why this class, and what we're actually trying to do out here. Because the boat isn't just a detail in this campaign - she's the whole foundation of it.

What is the Dehler 30 OD?

The Dehler 30 OD is a strict one-design class. That means every boat is the same - same hull, same keel, same rig, same sail plan. You can't swap in a lighter rudder or a taller mast or a fancier set of winches. The boat you buy is the boat you race. Full stop.

This matters more than people realise. In most offshore racing, the boat with the biggest budget usually wins. Better sails, better gear, better everything. One-design flips that on its head. When every boat is identical, the only variable left is the crew. How well you sail, how smart your tactics are, how you handle the boat when it's dark and rough and you haven't slept - that's what decides who finishes first.

When every boat is the same, the only thing left to optimise is you.

The class was designed by Judel/Vrolijk, the same naval architecture firm behind a long list of successful racing and cruising yachts. They drew a boat that's fast enough to be genuinely exciting, stable enough to be safe offshore, and simple enough that two people can handle it without a third pair of hands.

The key specs

Alliance is compact but she packs a lot into 30 feet. Here's what you're looking at:

Alliance at a glance

  • Length - 30 feet (9.14 m) overall
  • Displacement - 2,800 kg
  • Rig - carbon fibre mast and boom, fractional rig
  • Keel - deep fin keel with lead bulb for stability
  • Rudders - twin rudders for control at high heel angles
  • Sails - mainsail, jib, asymmetric spinnaker, code zero
  • Class rule - strict one-design, no modifications permitted

The carbon rig keeps the weight up top to a minimum, which means the boat is responsive and stiff without needing a huge, heavy keel to counterbalance it. The twin rudders mean that when the boat heels hard in a gust, the leeward rudder stays deep in the water and keeps giving you control - something a single rudder can't do as well at extreme angles.

At 2,800 kg, she's light for a 30-footer - about half the weight of a cruising yacht the same length. That means she accelerates quickly, surfs down waves, and responds immediately to the wind filling or dropping. It also means she moves around a lot. You feel everything on this boat. Every wave, every gust, every shift. That's part of what makes her fun to sail - and part of what makes her demanding.

What does "double-handed" actually mean?

Double-handed means two crew. That's it. Two people to do everything - sail changes, navigation, cooking, helming, sleeping, keeping watch, fixing whatever breaks at 3am. On a fully crewed boat you might have eight or ten people sharing those jobs. On Alliance, it's me and one other person, and the ocean doesn't care that we're short-staffed.

The ocean doesn't care that you're short-staffed.

Double-handed racing has exploded in popularity over the last decade, and for good reason. It's more accessible - you don't need to recruit and coordinate a crew of ten. It's more personal - every decision, every mistake, every good call is yours. And it's more demanding, which makes it more rewarding. There's nowhere to hide when there are only two of you.

The Dehler 30 OD was designed specifically for this. The sail handling systems are set up so one person can do everything from the cockpit. The autopilot integration means you can let the boat steer while you go forward to sort out a sail. The whole layout assumes two people, not ten. That's not a limitation - it's the point.

Why this boat?

I looked at a lot of options before landing on the Dehler 30 OD. Here's what tipped the scales.

The class is growing in Australia. There's a fleet building here, which means competition. A one-design boat with no one to race against is just an expensive daysailer. The fact that more boats are arriving, more crews are forming, and more races are adding Dehler 30 divisions - that's what makes this a real campaign and not a vanity project.

It's built for what we're doing. A lot of boats can go offshore. Not many are designed from scratch for two people to race offshore. The Dehler 30 OD is one of them. Every system, every line, every piece of hardware assumes a crew of two. We're not adapting a boat to our style of sailing - we're sailing a boat that was drawn for it.

One-design keeps it honest. I want to get better at sailing, not better at spending money. The strict class rules mean that the boat we have right now is the same boat we'll race in two years. The only thing that improves is us. That's motivating in a way that chasing the next equipment upgrade never is.

The campaign

So what are we actually doing with Alliance? Racing her offshore, starting with the Australian east coast.

The 2026 season is built around two key races: the Sydney to Gold Coast and the Gold Coast to Mackay. Both are proper offshore passages - hundreds of nautical miles up the coast, through the kinds of conditions that test everything: light air patience, heavy weather nerve, sleep deprivation, and the ability to keep making good decisions when you're tired and wet and the horizon won't stay still.

These races are the foundation. They're where we build the miles, the experience, and the record that opens the door to bigger events down the track. Every serious offshore campaign starts with races like these - learning the boat, learning each other, learning what works and what falls apart when you're 50 miles from shore at midnight.

Every serious offshore campaign starts the same way: miles, mistakes, and midnight.

What makes Alliance special

I could give you a technical answer about hull form and righting moment, but the honest answer is simpler than that. Alliance is special because she's the right boat for the job.

She's fast enough to be competitive. She's tough enough to go offshore. She's simple enough that two people can handle her without heroics. And she's part of a one-design class that means we're always racing on a level playing field.

That combination - speed, safety, simplicity, and fairness - is rare. Most boats give you some of those things. The Dehler 30 OD gives you all of them, and that's why she's the boat we're building this campaign around.

Everything you'll read on this site - the sail trim articles, the weather strategy, the race reports - starts with this boat. She's the platform. She's the classroom. And honestly, she's a pretty great teacher.
