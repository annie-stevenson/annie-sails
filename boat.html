Meet the boat

Employment Hero Alliance

A 9.4-metre carbon-rigged Dehler 30 One Design, built for the double-handed Olympic class. On loan from her owner for the season.

The specs

A boat designed for two.

The Dehler 30 OD was purpose-built in 2019 for the (eventually abandoned) double-handed Olympic class. The class is gone; the boats are still wonderful.

Her story

Borrowed, not bought.

Alliance belongs to Ben Thompson, who has very generously handed her over for the 2026 season. Ben has been campaigning her hard for years — there's not much we'll teach this boat that she hasn't already learned. She comes with hours of bow time, a known polar, and the kind of small-boat reputation that means you don't need to introduce yourself when she crosses a start line.

The deal is simple: take care of her, sail her well, return her in better shape than we found her.

Why this boat

Engineered for short-handed offshore.

Every line on Alliance leads to the cockpit. Every sail can be handled by one person. The boat has a square-top main, a self-tacking jib option, no spinnaker pole — all the design decisions are built around a simple constraint: at any moment, one of two people might be asleep.

That's the design problem of double-handed offshore racing in one sentence. Alliance solves it about as well as a 30-foot boat can.

  • Carbon spar — light enough to single-hand reef.
  • Retractable bowsprit — asymmetric goes up and down from the cockpit.
  • Twin rudders — keeps a grip when she's pressed at speed.
  • Generous bow volume — designed to plane downwind, not nose-dive.
